Peripheral Nervous System

The PNS consists of all the nerves that lie outside the brain and spinal cord. Its primary function is to connect the CNS to the limbs and organs, serving as a communication relay between the brain, spinal cord, and the rest of the body.



Sensory (Afferent) Nerves:
These neurons carry sensory information from the body's receptors (like those in the skin,
muscles, and organs) to the CNS. They are responsible for relaying sensations such as touch,
pain, temperature, and body position.



Motor (Efferent) Neurons:
Motor neurons transmit signals from the CNS to muscles and glands, initiating actions such as muscle contractions.
They are divided into two categories:
• Somatic Nervous System: Controls voluntary movements by activating skeletal muscles.

• Autonomic Nervous System: Regulates involuntary functions like heart rate, digestion, and respiratory rate.
The autonomic system itself is further divided into:

• Sympathetic Nervous System: Prepares the body for stress-related activities ("fight or flight" response).

• Parasympathetic Nervous System: a network of nerves that relaxes your body after periods of stress or danger.
